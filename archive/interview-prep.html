From Campus to Career · Interview Prep

Interview Prep Hub

Stop guessing what the interviewer wants. Start preparing with a system.

This page is built for students and early-career candidates preparing for internships, apprenticeships, new grad roles, and early full-time opportunities. Jose brings the student-side view of what it feels like to prepare in real time, and Jocelyn brings the early-career view of what actually stands out once you are in the room. Together, this page gives you practical tools, organized pathways, and interview-specific resources you can use right now.

Section 01

Start by Grade Level

Different stages. Different needs. Different resources.

Students at different points in college need completely different preparation. Find your stage, read the paragraph, and work through the recommended resources in order - that is the fastest path to being ready.

First-Year / Sophomore

First Interviews & Early Opportunities

If you are early in college, the goal is not to sound "impressive" - it is to show curiosity, reliability, communication, and proof that you can learn quickly. You may be interviewing for externships, insight programs, student leadership roles, or freshman/sophomore internships. You do not need a perfect story. You need a clear one.

Recommended resources

Tell Me About Yourself Script First Interview Checklist Common Behavioral Questions for Early Students How to Talk About Class Projects and Club Work Questions to Ask at the End of an Interview
Junior / Internship Recruiting

Internship Interviews & Recruiter Rounds

Juniors are usually being tested on readiness, communication, and whether you can contribute quickly in an internship environment. You need to know how to talk about projects, coursework, leadership, and prior experience in a structured way - not just describe what you did, but frame what it shows about you.

Recommended resources

STAR Story Bank Resume-to-Interview Translation Guide Recruiter Screen Prep Guide Technical Interview Checklist Behavioral Interview Question Bank Post-Interview Thank You Template
Senior / New Grad

Full-Time Roles, Finals & Late Cycle

Senior-level interviews often test maturity, clarity, decision-making, and whether you seem ready to operate as a full-time employee rather than just a student. This section also helps seniors still searching late in the cycle - the question is not just how to interview, but how to interview when your search timeline is longer than your peers'.

Recommended resources

Full-Time Tell Me About Yourself Script How to Explain Your Search Timeline Final Round Prep Guide Offer-Stage Interview Questions Questions to Ask Hiring Managers Post-Interview Reflection Doc
Recent Grad / Bridge Year

Gap Navigation, Apprenticeships & Transitions

This stage is less about sounding perfect and more about explaining your path clearly, showing momentum, and connecting your past experience to where you are trying to go next. Interviewers will ask about your timeline. The way you answer that question matters more than most candidates realize.

Section 02

Browse by Interview Type

I have a [type] interview tomorrow. Here is exactly where to start.

Recruiter screens are usually the first filter and are less about technical depth than about clarity, fit, communication, and whether your story makes sense. The recruiter is not trying to trip you up - they are trying to decide if they should send you further. Your job is to be clear, specific, and easy to advocate for. Practice your "tell me about yourself" and your "why this company" until they are effortless.

Resources for this round

Recruiter Screen Prep Checklist 10 Common Recruiter Screen Questions Tell Me About Yourself Script Why This Role / Why This Company Framework Salary Question Guidance for Early-Career

Behavioral interviews are used to predict future performance based on past actions - which is why the STAR method matters so much. Every question that starts with "tell me about a time when…" is an invitation to tell a story. The candidates who do well in behavioral rounds are not the ones with the most impressive stories; they are the ones who tell their stories with the most structure and specificity.

Resources for this round

STAR Story Bank Behavioral Question Bank (30 questions) Story Selection Guide "Weaknesses" Answer Framework Leadership / Teamwork / Failure Answer Builder

Technical interviews test problem-solving, clarity of thought, and communication under pressure - not just whether you get the perfect answer. Interviewers often care as much about how you think out loud as about what you produce. If you get stuck, narrating your thought process and asking clarifying questions will always serve you better than going silent and hoping.

Resources for this round

Technical Interview Prep Checklist How to Narrate Your Thinking Out Loud Project Walkthrough Framework SQL / Coding / Data Question Prep Templates What to Do When You Get Stuck

Case interviews test structure, logic, prioritization, and communication rather than memorized knowledge. They are most common in consulting, product, strategy, and business internships. The biggest mistake candidates make is jumping to conclusions before structuring the problem. The interviewers are evaluating your process - how you break down ambiguous problems - more than your final answer.

Resources for this round

Case Interview Framework Sheet Profitability / Market Entry / Product Strategy Examples How to Structure Your Answer in Real Time Common Mistakes in Case Interviews

Asynchronous and recorded interviews are increasingly common - and they need their own strategy. Timing, tone, environment, and on-camera presence matter differently when there is no human on the other side reacting to you. The biggest traps are reading answers off a script, running over time, and a poor setup that distracts from your answers.

Resources for this round

One-Way Interview Checklist Camera / Lighting / Environment Setup Guide How to Sound Natural Without Reading Time-Boxed Answer Practice Sheet

Final round interviews often involve hiring managers, team members, and sometimes senior leadership. They are testing maturity, team fit, communication style, and readiness for the actual day-to-day work. The questions are less predictable than earlier rounds - interviewers at this stage want to have a conversation, not run a checklist. The candidates who advance are the ones who seem genuinely ready to show up.

Resources for this round
